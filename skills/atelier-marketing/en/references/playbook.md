# Positioning and offer framing

Everything you write — content, a campaign, a landing page — rests on a clear
positioning. These principles feed the other two workflows; they don't
replace voice (`brand-voice.md`) or campaign structure (`campaigns.md`).

## The positioning sentence

One sentence, before anything else gets written:

> For <type of buyer>, who need <specific pain or need>, <offer> is a
> <category> that <main benefit> — unlike <alternative>, we <provable
> difference>.

If the executive can't fill in this sentence for the offer at hand, nothing
is ready to write yet — go fill the blank with them first, never on their
behalf.

## The "so what" test

Every feature has to survive "so what, for my customer?". "Ships in 48
hours" isn't a benefit until the answer to "so what" is spelled out: "so you
don't lose the sale while you wait." Always write the answer, not just the
feature.

## One audience, one offer, one message

Resist writing to everyone at once. An email trying to win over both a loyal
customer and a cold lead convinces neither — it just dilutes. Pick the
audience before the first line gets written; other audiences get their own
piece, not a paragraph bolted onto this one.

## Proof beats adjectives

"Best," "revolutionary," "seamless" prove nothing — any competitor can claim
the same words. A number, a met deadline, a before/after, a customer count:
that's what actually persuades. Swap every superlative for the proof that
would back it up, or cut it.

## The substitution test

Reread the line and ask: could a competitor say this exact sentence? If yes,
it says nothing specific to this company — rewrite until it fails that test.
This is the fastest way to catch generic copy before it goes out.

## Frame by outcome, not mechanism

Open with what the customer gets, not how it was built. "Our platform runs
on proprietary technology" interests whoever built it, not the customer
wondering what changes for them. The mechanism can follow, as proof — never
as the opening line.

## One call to action, not three

Copy asking a reader to subscribe, reply, and buy in the same breath drives
none of the three. Pick the one action that matters most for this piece and
cut the rest — they can live in the next piece.
