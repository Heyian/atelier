# Campaign structure

A campaign isn't a stack of copy — it's one audience, one offer, a
measurable goal, and a sequence over time.

## Four things settled before anything gets written

- **One audience** — a single audience per campaign. Two very different
  audiences earn two campaigns, not one piece trying to please both.
- **One offer** — exactly what the customer gets, with its terms (length,
  code, quantity). A fuzzy offer produces fuzzy copy.
- **A measurable goal** — not "raise awareness," but "20 booked calls" or
  "50 signups." Without a starting number, nobody can tell afterward whether
  the campaign worked.
- **A launch date** — the anchor the rest of the calendar hangs off.

Load `playbook.md` for the positioning sentence that feeds every piece of
the campaign, and `brand-voice.md` to score each piece before it ships.

## Sequence channels, never fire them all at once

Sending everything the same day saturates the audience and makes it
impossible to tell which channel did the work. Sequence instead:

1. **Primary channel** — carries the full message, with all the
   explanation (usually the newsletter or a dedicated page).
2. **Supporting channels** — repeat fragments of the primary message, not a
   new version: a social post that reuses the same hook, a reminder that
   reuses the deadline.
3. **Closing reminder** — near the deadline, on whichever channel performed
   best in the earlier steps.

Sample calendar to adapt, never to copy as-is:

| Day | Channel | Content |
|---|---|---|
| Day -14 | Newsletter | Full announcement, offer, and dates |
| Day -7 | Social | Short reminder, same hook |
| Day 0 | Newsletter | Launch, single call to action |
| Day +3 | Social | Social proof or a question to the audience |
| Day +7 | Newsletter | Final reminder before the deadline |

## The review gate — it belongs to the executive

Before anything sends or publishes, the executive signs off. This isn't a
formality to skip for speed — it's the moment they decide whether the tone,
the offer, and the calendar are what they actually want in front of their
customers. Name that moment explicitly in the plan — which piece, which
date, who gives the go-ahead — and never press send on their behalf.

## After launch

Measure against the number set at the start, not against how it felt. A
campaign that generated activity but missed its number isn't a hidden
failure — it's data for the next one: which channel carried it, which
channel added nothing, what to adjust before the next sequence.
