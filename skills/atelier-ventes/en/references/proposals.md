# Building a proposal

A proposal that could go to any customer convinces none of them. It has to
read like an answer to this specific deal, not a template filled in a
hurry.

A proposal leaves the building with the executive's name on it: write it in
the Company Profile's Tone of voice, using the executive's own Vocabulary —
their words for their offer, their market, their services. Before delivering
it, reread it term by term against the profile's Vocabulary. Where a generic
word sits in place of a house term, the house term wins.

## Anatomy of a proposal

Five blocks, in this order — each answers a question the customer is
already asking while reading:

1. **The problem, in their own words** — restate what the customer said
   about their situation, not what's being sold. A customer who recognizes
   themselves in the first paragraph keeps reading.
2. **The proposed solution** — what gets delivered, concretely, without
   internal jargon.
3. **Scope** — what's included, and just as important, what isn't. A fuzzy
   scope produces a dispute later, never an easier sale today.
4. **The investment** — price, terms, and what makes it move (options,
   volume, timeline). Never a vague "starting at" figure without saying
   what it depends on.
5. **Next step** — one requested action, with a date. A proposal that ends
   without a specific action ends without a reply.

## The approved-facts rule

Nothing in a proposal gets invented — never a customer count, never a
price, never a named reference without the executive's explicit go-ahead
to use it. A customer who catches one invented number in a proposal stops
trusting the rest of the document, even the parts that were accurate.

The rule in practice:

- **A price** always comes from the executive, never copied from an older
  proposal without checking it still holds, never a "reasonable" guess.
- **A customer count or a rate** (satisfaction, retention, average
  turnaround) comes from a number the executive confirms for this specific
  document — not a rough memory of an earlier conversation.
- **A named reference** (a customer, a case study) needs explicit
  permission for this customer, in this document — permission given for a
  different proposal doesn't carry over automatically.
- **A missing fact stays visible** — write `[confirm: price per unit]` in
  the draft rather than filling the gap with a plausible-sounding number. A
  visible gap gets fixed in thirty seconds; an invented number gets
  discovered mid-negotiation.

## One customer, one proposal

Recycling the last proposal almost as-is shows — a name half-changed, a
scope that doesn't match what this customer actually asked for. Start from
the structure, never from a previous proposal's text copied over; what
repeats across deals is the five blocks, not the sentences.

## Before delivering

Reread the document tracing every number, every name, every reference back
to its source — and for each one, confirm it: said by the executive in this
conversation, or already written in the Company Profile. Anything whose
source is neither is something to remove or confirm before delivering,
never something to wave through because "it sounded about right."
