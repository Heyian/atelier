# Reading a pipeline

A pipeline isn't a list of dollar amounts — it's a set of bets on the
future, each with a probability you can check instead of guess at.

## A stage is a verifiable fact, not a feeling

"It's moving along" isn't a stage. A stage earns its place because of a
fact you can name: a proposal sent, a decision date the customer confirmed,
a signature received. If nobody can say which fact moved a deal to the next
stage, it probably never left the previous one — and the review should put
it back where the facts say it belongs, not where everyone wishes it were.

## The "since when" test

One question settles most of a deal's health: since when has it sat at this
stage, and since when has nobody heard back? A deal marked "in negotiation"
for two months with no recent reply isn't in negotiation — it's dead or
asleep, and the two need different handling. Set a threshold per stage (say,
ten days of silence means it's due for a follow-up, thirty means it's due
for triage) and apply it consistently instead of judging each deal by feel.

## Triaging stalled deals

Three questions per stalled deal, in order:

1. **What's the actual blocker?** A price to revisit, a decision stuck in a
   committee, a contact who changed roles. A named blocker can be worked;
   an unnamed one just gets guessed at.
2. **Whose court is the ball in?** If it's been sitting in the customer's
   court for weeks with no follow-up sent, that's not a stalled deal — it's
   a dropped one.
3. **What's the next action, with a date attached?** A deal with no dated
   next action isn't being managed, it's just parked. Every deal that stays
   in the pipeline leaves this triage with an action and a date, or it
   leaves the pipeline.

## What a good review surfaces

A good review doesn't stop at listing deals — it answers questions the
executive hasn't had time to ask:

- **Concentration risk** — is a single deal carrying half the target
  number? Losing it changes everything; say that plainly, don't bury it.
- **Stage bunching** — too many deals stuck at the same stage usually
  points to a process problem, not five separate deal problems.
- **Deals with no next action** — the most reliable early signal that a
  deal is about to die quietly.
- **How real the total actually is** — a pipeline total that counts a deal
  asleep for two months the same as a hot one tells you nothing useful;
  separate what's likely from what's theoretical.

## Data hygiene, not just deal hygiene

A regular review is also the moment to flag what's polluting the pipeline
itself: two records for the same customer under slightly different names,
a stage that no longer matches what actually happened, a required field
left blank that makes a deal invisible in reporting. Flag these as they
come up rather than skipping them as "not today's topic" — a dirty CRM
makes every future review less trustworthy.
