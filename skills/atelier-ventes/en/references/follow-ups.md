# Drafting a follow-up

A follow-up that just says "wanted to check in" reads like an invoice
reminder. It only earns the customer's time if it brings something the last
message didn't.

## Rhythm, not guesswork

Following up too soon reads as if you have nothing else going on. Waiting
too long lets the deal go cold and hands the customer a good excuse to
assume you forgot. A useful default rhythm, to adjust to the company's
actual sales cycle:

| Follow-up | Time since last contact | What it should add |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | 3–5 business days | A concrete addition: an answer to something left open, a relevant example |
| 2nd | 10–14 days | A different angle: a comparable case, a real deadline that's now closer |
| 3rd | 3–4 weeks | A polite exit: confirm whether it's still a priority, no guilt trip |

## Every follow-up earns its send

"Just checking in" gives the customer no new reason to reply this time
versus last time. Always bring a fact, a specific question, or something
that's genuinely changed since the last exchange: the answer to what was
left open, a comparable customer example (with permission to name it), a
real deadline coming up. With nothing new to say, it's often more useful to
wait until there is something than to send an empty message.

## Tone tracks the silence, not the mood of the moment

A first follow-up stays warm and assumes good faith — the person is
probably busy, not uninterested. A later one can name the silence directly
and offer an easy exit rather than pushing harder: "If now's not the right
time, just say so and I'll close this out on my end" often gets a real
answer where a fourth polite nudge gets nothing.

## The facts rule — never invent anything to strengthen a message

A punchy number is tempting to add — a customer count, a satisfaction
stat, a named reference. None of it gets invented, not even
approximately, not even to "add weight" to the message. If it isn't
confirmed by the executive or already sitting in the Company Profile, the
message goes out without it, or waits for confirmation before it sends.

## The line that never moves: drafts are AI, sending is the executive's

Drafting the follow-up is part of this skill's job. Sending it never is.
Always deliver copy-ready or edit-ready text, never a claim that it went
out — even when the executive is in a hurry. The call to hit send, with the
exact tone and timing, belongs to them alone.
