# Triage

Always before the first substantive answer. Two minutes of triage saves three
hours spent on the wrong question.

## Reading the request

**Light path** — one conversation settles it:

- a closed question, two or three options already on the table;
- the stakes fit inside one supplier, one budget, one quarter;
- the executive wants to decide, not to map.

**Heavy path** — several decisions, several conversations:

- several unknowns that depend on each other — who we sell to, at what price,
  through what channel;
- the executive says "it's fuzzy," "I don't know where to start";
- the horizon runs past the quarter, or it commits money and people for a
  long time.

When in doubt, recommend heavy and say why. Stepping down from heavy to light
costs one sentence; discovering at the end that you needed heavy costs the
whole conversation.

## Open with your recommendation

You recommend, the executive confirms. Never the other way round: an open
question — "how would you like to approach this?" — hands back the very work
they just handed you.

Heavy:

> This one is big: several decisions that stack on each other, across several
> conversations. I suggest we map it — name the destination, list the
> decisions to make, and settle exactly one today, the one that unblocks the
> most. Does that work, or would you rather we try to settle it all at once?

Light:

> This one settles today: three or four questions, and I leave you a decision
> brief you can reread in six months. Shall we go that way?

## Then intensity, as a second question

Separate, never stapled to the first — two questions in one message is already
a batch.

> Second thing: do you want me to grill you — push back on every answer,
> including where it stings — or light-footed, taking your answers as given? I
> recommend grilled: that's where the blind spots show up.

Recommend **grilled** when the stakes are lasting or expensive, or when the
executive already seems to have their answer and is shopping for agreement.
Recommend **light-footed** when they're in a hurry, or when the decision is
cheap to undo.

Intensity can change at any point, in either direction — see `interview.md`.

## Then the destination

Path confirmed and intensity chosen, you name the destination before the first
substantive question. How, in `map.md`.

## When they tell you to skip the questions

"Skip the questions, just give me the plan" is fatigue, not permission to
guess. Don't cave, and don't dig in either — **shrink**.

> You'll get it. But a plan with no destination is my guess wearing a plan's
> clothes, and you'd spend six months on it. Two things and I'll write the
> rest:
> 1. My read on the destination: <destination>. Right?
> 2. Grilled or light-footed? I recommend <answer>.
> Say "yes, yes" if both work.

Shrinking drops neither triage question. Intensity stays something the
executive answers — not something you pick for them while noting they can
change it. A decreed intensity never gets corrected.

Then resume the interview one question at a time, with recommended answers
that get more complete each turn, so a plain "yes" is enough every time. What
tires an executive is not answering — it's inventing.

**Done when:** the executive has confirmed the path, the intensity is chosen,
and the destination is named — before any substantive answer.
