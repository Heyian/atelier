# Company Profile example

An invented company. It doesn't exist; the names and numbers are fictional.
What matters here is the grain: nine sections, in order, written in the
executive's own words — not in brochure English.

A good profile fits on two screens and can be read aloud without wincing.

# Company Profile — Ambrose Orchard Systems

_Last updated: 2026-07-21_

## Role

Owner and general manager. I decide pricing, hiring, and anything that commits
us past $40k on my own. Roughly half my week is on the road with growers, the
other half in the shop. What I don't decide alone: warranty calls over $5k —
those go to Dale, because he's the one who has to eat them.

## Company

Ambrose Orchard Systems. Started in 1998 by my father, I took over in 2019.
Twenty-two people, one shop outside Yakima, two service trucks that never sit
still. No outside investors, no debt beyond the trucks. We've never had a
layoff and I don't intend to be the one who starts.

## Offer

We sell and service **orchard equipment** for mid-size fruit growers — apples,
cherries, pears.

Three lines of business:

- **Iron** — new and used platforms, sprayers, and bin trailers. Big tickets,
  thin margin, and it's what gets us in the door.
- **Service** — scheduled maintenance contracts and emergency repair. Half our
  revenue and nearly all our profit.
- **Parts counter** — walk-in and overnight. Small, but it's the reason a
  grower calls us first at 5 a.m.

We also do something we've never charged for: we help growers work out what
their spray program actually needs before we quote them equipment. It's usually
what wins the deal.

## Market

Our typical customer: a family operation running 200 to 1,200 acres in central
Washington or northern Oregon, second or third generation, where the person
signing is the owner or the orchard manager — never a purchasing department.

Who we're up against: the two national dealers who can undercut us on new iron
by 8% and have a financing arm we can't match, and a handful of independent
mechanics who beat us on service price but can't get parts inside a week. Our
argument is always the same one: a truck on your block within twenty-four
hours, and the same three people who know your equipment.

## Tone of voice

Plain, unhurried, never salesy. Growers can smell a pitch from the parking lot.

Three adjectives: straight, practical, unhurried.

Words we never use: "solutions", "partner" as a verb, "cutting-edge". If a
sentence could have come from any equipment dealer in the country, rewrite it.

We use first names with everyone, and we don't send anything that reads like it
was written by a committee.

## Priorities

This quarter, three things and nothing else:

1. Get 15 growers onto annual service contracts before harvest — right now
   they're all on call-and-hope.
2. Cut quote turnaround from nine days to two. We are losing deals to slower
   competitors purely because they answer faster.
3. Hire a second service coordinator. Dale is doing two jobs and it shows.

## Team context

- **Dale Ferriter**, service manager. Owns the trucks, the schedule, and
  warranty. Been here since 2004 and knows every machine in the valley.
- **Rosa Cardenas**, parts and inside sales. Owns the counter, inventory, and
  every overnight order. She is the voice most customers actually hear.
- **Beth Ambrose**, bookkeeper, three days a week. Owns invoicing, payroll, and
  telling me no.

There's no marketing function. It's me and Rosa, between other things — which
is exactly what I'm hoping to change.

## Vocabulary

- **Iron / Service / Parts counter** — our three lines of business. That's how
  we talk about them internally, and it's how the numbers are broken out.
- **A block** — a contiguous planting of one variety. Growers say "the Honeycrisp
  block", never "field" and never "orchard section".
- **PHI** — pre-harvest interval, the days that must pass between a spray and
  picking. It drives every service window we schedule in August.
- **A quote**, never a "proposal". A proposal is something a consultant sends.
- **A cold account** — a customer who hasn't bought in twelve months. We work
  the cold list every January.
- **Blue tag** — our internal label for a used machine we've reconditioned and
  will warranty. Customers know the term; it means something to them.

## AI ambitions

What eats the most of my week: quotes. Every request comes in differently — a
voicemail, a text with a photo, a note handed to Rosa at the counter — and we
start from scratch every time. Nine days is our worst leak.

What I'd like off my plate, in order:

1. First drafts of quotes, built from our price list and what that account has
   bought before.
2. Notes from the Monday shop meeting. Nobody writes them, so decisions
   evaporate and we relitigate them three weeks later.
3. Follow-ups to the cold list, written the way we actually talk — not like a
   marketing email.

What success looks like: a quote out the door in two days, and me spending
Tuesday on somebody's block instead of in my inbox.

What I don't want handed to AI: price conversations with a grower we've had for
twenty years, and anything that touches a warranty decision.
