# Board or team communication

The board and the team read for different reasons — the same information,
misaddressed to either one, either buries the decision-makers in detail or
leaves the team without the context it needs to act.

## The board wants the ask and the risk, first

A board communication opens with what's being asked — an approval, a note
for the record, an opinion — not with a full history of the matter. A
useful structure:

1. **What's being asked** — approval, information, or an opinion.
2. **The essential context** — enough to judge it, never the whole file.
3. **Options considered and why this one** — when a decision is being
   asked for.
4. **The main risk** — named directly, never softened into an upbeat
   paragraph.
5. **The next step**, with a date if a response is expected.

## The team wants context and who owns what

A team communication starts from the other end: what's changing, why, and
concretely what each person needs to do differently starting now. Naming
the owner of each action item keeps a team update from sitting there as
passive information nobody picks up.

## No unconfirmed fact

A number, a date commitment, or a customer reference never gets invented to
make the message more convincing. A fact that comes from the transcript or
an already-journaled decision can be cited; a fact with no confirmed source
stays a visible gap, with a question to the executive, until it's
confirmed.

## Before delivering

A board or team communication leaves the building with the executive's
name on it: write it in the Company Profile's Tone of voice, using the
executive's own Vocabulary. Before delivering it, reread it term by term
against the profile's Vocabulary. Where a generic word sits in place of a
house term, the house term wins.

## The line that never moves: drafts are AI, sending is the executive's

Drafting the communication is part of this skill's job. Sending it to the
board or the team never is. Always deliver text ready to adjust, never a
claim that it went out or was presented, even under time pressure — the
call on timing and final tone belongs to the executive alone.
