# Minutes — anatomy and rules

Minutes aren't a recap of what got said — they're a document that lets
someone who wasn't there know exactly what was decided, by whom, and what's
still open. They should read in five minutes, even six months later.

## Anatomy of minutes — five parts, always

1. **Date** — the date of the meeting. A date that appears nowhere in the
   transcript stays a visible gap marked `[date to confirm]`, never a date
   guessed from today: a raw transcript often gives timestamps ("09:02")
   but never the day, month, or year.
2. **Attendees** — who was there (and, when useful, who was absent or
   excused).
3. **Topics discussed** — every item covered, with enough context that
   someone who wasn't there understands what it was about. A topic still
   under debate belongs here too, flagged as such — see the rule below.
4. **Decisions taken** — every decision actually settled, worded as a
   decision ("we're renewing at...", "we're confirming...") never as a
   direction or an intention.
5. **Action items** — each one with a named owner and a due date. An action
   with no named owner isn't an action, it's a wish.

## The rule that matters most: an unresolved debate stays unresolved

A conversation that circles a topic without ever landing on it often feels
like it converged — the tone settles, everyone seems to agree on the
general direction. That isn't a decision. The test: does some sentence in
the transcript state plainly what was settled, by whom, and as of when? If
not, it's an open item: it belongs under "Topics discussed," never under
"Decisions taken." Name what's blocking it, what's missing to settle it, and
when it comes back on an agenda. Promoting a debate into a decision because
the conversation seemed to be trending one way is the costliest mistake
minutes can make — it tells the whole organization a call was made when it
wasn't.

## Every decision, proposed to the journal — never written directly

A leadership decision found in the transcript never gets written directly
to `{root}/docs/atelier/decisions.md`. It gets proposed first: a short
summary — the decision, the why in plain language, as it comes out of the
transcript — then wait for the executive's agreement. A request to log
everything at once, even framed as a gesture of trust, doesn't change the
rule: each decision stays a separate proposal, never a batch write with no
review. The entry has to be self-sufficient — readable on its own, with no
need to go dig up the minutes; a pointer to the minutes is a bonus, never
the only place the reasoning lives. See `references/memory-protocol.md` for
the full mechanics.

## Out of scope for v1

Deep minutes specialization — legal formats, board templates with precise
governance requirements — goes beyond what this skill covers. For that,
offer to build a custom skill with `atelier-forge` rather than forcing this
generic template to meet requirements it doesn't know about.
